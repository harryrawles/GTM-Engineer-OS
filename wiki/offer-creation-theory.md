# Offer Creation Theory

The psychology behind why a stranger converts into a client, and what separates a "grand slam" offer
from an average one. This file is the **theory** - it explains *why* the process works.
[wiki/offer-framework.md](./offer-framework.md) is the **applied 12-step process** - use it to *build and
validate* an offer. Read this file first if a step in that process feels arbitrary; it almost certainly
maps to a principle below.

Primary methodology (per `CLAUDE.md` → Source Knowledge) - provided by Harry Rawles, credited to Alex
Hormozi's pricing/offer work where noted.

## 1. The Six Pillars of Client Acquisition

Converting a stranger into a client requires six things:

| Pillar | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| **Drive** | The unconscious driving force of human behaviour - the root reason for any action or decision (to get or escape something) |
| **Goal** | A future situation the person wants to live in, manifest, or see come true |
| **Problem** | An obstacle standing between them and their goal |
| **Pain** | The unpleasant feeling created by the problem |
| **Action** | The mental decision and physical behaviour taken to alleviate pain |
| **Confidence** | Faith, belief, and trust in someone (or a company) to solve the problem |

**The causal chain:**

```
Drive > Goal > Problem > Pain > Action
```

Action additionally *requires* Confidence - pain alone does not produce action without it.

**Rule 1 of client acquisition:** focus on the six pillars. **Rule 2:** don't forget rule 1.

---

## 2. What the Stranger Brings vs What You Must Create

The stranger already has four of the six pillars - you only need to supply the rest.

| Pillar | Who creates it |
|--------|----------------|
| Drive | Pre-built into the stranger - already exists |
| Goal | Comes from their drive |
| Problem | Comes from their goal |
| Pain (baseline) | Comes from their problem |
| Action (attempted) | Comes from pain, but rarely fires without help |
| **Confidence** | **You** - by seeming competent, capable, reliable, trustworthy |
| **Pain (amplified)** | **You** - by exploring and exposing pain that already exists |
| **Action (elicited)** | **You** - by encouraging and prompting the decision |

Two governing rules:

> **Rule:** you do not create pain - you expose and amplify pain that already exists. Pain rarely drives
> action without amplification, because people enter delusions to cope with reality; pain hurts, so it
> gets denied or minimised until someone surfaces it.

> **Rule:** you do not create action - you encourage and elicit it. People rarely act to escape pain or
> solve a problem without an external trigger or nudge.

## 3. Pools of Confidence

Confidence is a measure of someone's belief in your ability to solve their problem. A stranger can draw
confidence from three places ("pools"):

| Pool | Source |
|------|--------|
| **1. Your offer** | How your product is positioned as a vehicle to alleviate pain, solve the problem, and satisfy the underlying drive |
| **2. You** | Your own unwavering, genuine conviction that you can help them achieve their goal |
| **3. Your clients** | Social proof - reviews, reputation, case studies, client interviews, other people like them you've already solved this for |

It doesn't matter which pool the confidence comes from - only that they get enough of it. **The offer is
the pool with the most leverage**: it's the easiest one to engineer deliberately, which is why offer
creation is worth disproportionate time relative to almost anything else in client acquisition.

---

## 4. Pools of Pain

Pain is a measure of someone's suffering in relation to part of their life. A stranger can draw pain from
five places:

| Pool | Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Problems** | Conscious | Obstacles standing between them and a goal | Appointment booking, dieting |
| **2. Unmet needs** | Unconscious | Core unfulfilled drives | Love, progress, attention, status, family |
| **3. HWIW** (having what isn't wanted) | Unconscious | Stuck in an unwanted situation | Debt, excess body fat, loneliness |
| **4. WWIH** (wanting what isn't had) | Unconscious | Desiring a situation they don't have | $1M, abs, a relationship |
| **5. Consequences** | Conscious | Suffering caused by any of the above going unsolved | Being broke, feeling lethargic, being unloved |

**Problems and Consequences are the easiest pools to extract pain from** because they're conscious -
people can articulate them directly. The unconscious pools (2-4) still cause real pain, but people can't
accurately attribute it, so they misattribute it to their conscious problems and consequences instead.
Explore the problem and its consequences thoroughly and you'll surface pain from all five pools at once.

### Amplifying pain - indirectly, always

Pain must never be amplified by bluntly telling someone they're in pain - it has to be discreet. Four
indirect techniques:

1. **Ask questions** that create pain by the nature of the person answering them.
2. **Explore the problem** - the act of exploring it, not just naming it, creates pain.
3. **Discuss consequences** they are already experiencing because of the situation.
4. **Create seriousness** - explain the weight of the situation and what happens if it continues.

> "So you're currently generating no new sales for the business - why is that?"
> "You've told me you can't get clients - can you expand on this for me?"
> "What's the knock-on effect of this problem in your life?"
> "If this isn't fixed and it gets worse, what does that mean for your business?"

These apply equally to sales calls and appointment-booking copy.

**On the ethics of this:** amplifying pain deliberately can sound manipulative in the abstract. The
working frame is the same one a doctor uses - if someone needs to face short-term discomfort to escape
long-term suffering, surfacing that discomfort is the responsible thing to do, not the exploitative one.
See `wiki/psychological-principles.md` (Pain-avoiding denial, Doubt avoidance) for the bias mechanics
underneath why people default to avoidance in the first place.

## 5. Action Thresholds

Every prospect has an action threshold - the point at which they convert from stranger to client. It's a
model, not an objective law, but it's useful for reasoning about pain and confidence together:

```
(Pain % + Confidence %) / 2 = % chance of action
```

Example: `(60% pain + 75% confidence) / 2 = 67.5% chance of action`. If their personal action threshold
sits below that number, they act.

**Practical implication:** a prospect "on the fence" is sitting just below their threshold. There is no
way to measure the exact number, but the lever is always the same - increase pain, increase confidence,
or both, and you move them across it. A sufficiently strong offer can lift confidence enough to clear the
threshold for an entire market at once, not just one prospect.

One of the fastest ways to move both numbers is **inversion**: remove doubt (confidence's absence) and
remove comfort (pain's absence). Confidence is a lack of doubt; pain is a lack of comfort.

## 6. The Gap

People have a current situation and a desired situation across every major area of life - health,
wealth, relationships, happiness. The entire economy runs on people buying things that move them from the
former to the latter. That distance is **the gap**.

Your product must be positioned as the vehicle - the staircase - that lets the prospect climb from their
current situation to their desired one. This is the theory behind why `wiki/offer-framework.md` Step 1
asks "what is their current situation?" and "what is their desired situation?" before anything else gets
written: those two questions define the gap your offer needs to bridge.

## 7. Offer Power

Why the offer, specifically, is worth this much attention:

| Effect | What it means |
|--------|--------------|
| **Rising tide** | A strong offer improves every downstream metric at once - sales conversion, show-up rate, booking rate - for a small, one-time input of effort. It's the single most asymmetric lever available. |
| **Persuasion trade-off** | The stronger the offer, the less persuasive skill is needed to close. Persuasion takes years to build; a good offer takes a few hours to construct and can outperform it. |
| **Making it easy** | A sufficiently strong offer makes saying no feel irrational - like the prospect would be acting against their own interest by declining. |
| **Exponent** | Offer strength compounds rather than adds. Rough scale: a bad offer ≈ `2¹ = 2`, an OK offer ≈ `2³ = 8`, a genuinely strong offer ≈ `2⁹ = 512`. |

An offer is composed of seven elements, presented at three levels of detail. This is *what* goes into an
offer; `wiki/offer-framework.md` is *how* you build each part - use the table below to jump straight to
the relevant step instead of duplicating the process here.

| Composition element | Definition | Built in |
|---------------------|------------|----------|
| Outcome | The promise - aligned to the goal/desired situation | offer-framework.md Step 2 |
| Timeframe | How long it takes to deliver the outcome | offer-framework.md Step 3 |
| Method | The tangible methodology that achieves the outcome | offer-framework.md Step 4 |
| Secrets | Your unique way of executing the method | Not a dedicated step - closest is Step 9 (Packaging). Consider this a known gap in the process, not the theory. |
| Safety net | Risk reversal / guarantee | offer-framework.md Step 6 |
| Polarising | Who it explicitly excludes | offer-framework.md Step 7 |
| Pricing | What it costs to claim | offer-framework.md Step 10 |

Three forms of the same offer, in increasing detail:

| Form | Length | Built in |
|------|--------|----------|
| Short-form message | 3-5 words, the core message | offer-framework.md Step 11 |
| Long-form message | A paragraph with key information | offer-framework.md Step 12 |
| The pitch | Full explanation of outcome, method, and terms | Not a dedicated step - this is the synthesis of Steps 2-10 delivered conversationally (sales call, proposal, landing page) |

## 9. Common Offer Mistakes

| Mistake | Why it fails |
|---------|-------------|
| **Copycatting** | Copying a competitor's offer is a viable way to start but not to scale - it caps you at their ceiling and gives the market no reason to choose you over them. Position as a market of one. |
| **Playing it safe** | The bolder the offer, the more confidence it inspires. An offer with real skin-in-the-game consequences for you (not just the client) signals genuine commitment and builds trust faster than a safe one. |
| **Complicating it** | Complicated offers confuse people, and confusion loses deals. Think MVO - minimum viable offer. The best way to strengthen an offer is usually to remove something from it, not add to it. |
| **Non-industry-specific** | An offer using generic language instead of the market's actual vocabulary alienates the market, even when the substance is right. Match the vernacular (see `wiki/industry-vernacular.md`). |
| **Based on product** | Never build the offer around what you can already deliver - build the product around the boldest offer the market actually wants. Limiting claims to existing skillset caps growth; letting claims stretch the skillset forces improvement. |

## 10. The Product-Offer Relationship

Two uncomfortable, related truths:

> The harder it is to build the product, the easier it is to acquire clients.
> The easier it is to build the product, the harder it is to acquire clients.

Making client acquisition easy means making product development hard - that's the trade-off, not a
coincidence. A product that costs real time, effort, and difficulty to build produces a stronger offer,
because it inspires more confidence on both sides: you have more conviction in something you struggled to
build, and clients feel more of that effort in the results, which builds more confidence in turn.

## 11. Pricing Philosophy

(Credit: Alex Hormozi, *$100M Offers* - adapted here as the pricing layer of offer theory.)

### Price-to-value discrepancy

Buyers act when they believe the value received clearly exceeds the price paid. The goal is to maximise
that gap:

- **Reduce price** - technically works, but price floors at zero and every reduction shrinks your margin.
- **Increase value** - the better lever. Value has no ceiling, and every increase strengthens the
  discrepancy without costing you a discount.

If a buyer feels value dropped below price *after* purchasing, the relationship - and referral potential
- is over. Most underperforming offers sit at or below this line.

### The virtuous cycle of price (the trap most businesses fall into)

The default pattern: look at competitors → take the average price → undercut it slightly to seem
"competitive" → offer a bit more for less → repeat. Every competitor in the market does this
simultaneously, margins erode across the board, and the whole market race to the bottom. This is **market
efficiency**, and it is why most businesses barely survive.

The alternative: raise the price. A drastically higher price forces the market to conclude "this must be
different," which is the opposite of the race-to-the-bottom trap.

### High price signals high value

Price is a communication of value - people genuinely perceive a more expensive product as better,
independent of its actual features. Hormozi's GymLaunch charged ~$15k against a market ceiling of ~$5k and
used the price gap itself as a differentiator. Higher price also gives you more capital per client to
reinvest in actually delivering the result.

**Run this thought experiment on any offer:** *if I 10x'd the price today, what would I need to add to
make it worth that?* (Directly extends the "if you 2x'd / 5x'd / 10x'd your price" prompts already in
`wiki/offer-framework.md` Step 10 - this section is the reasoning behind why that exercise works.)

## 12. The Art of Resonance - Latent Conditions

The highest-leverage version of offer/message creation isn't persuasion - it's resonance.

Think of it like drilling for oil: certain points on the Earth's surface sit above pressurised oil
deposits, and striking the right point releases it. The human mind has equivalent pressure points -
**latent conditions**: the underlying psychological, situational, and cognitive forces a market shares.
Find the right words, phrases, or images and you resonate with the unconscious mind of the market at its
existing "frequency" - triggering action without needing to persuade at all.

Marketing copy is the radio; latent conditions are the stations. Tune to the right frequency and the
prospect feels *understood*, and feeling understood is one of the fastest routes to trust (see
`wiki/psychological-principles.md` - Subjective validation, Understanding bias, Relatability).

**Example latent conditions (early-stage marketing agency owners):**

- Bought a $997 course from someone they admired, was disappointed, but still admires that person
- Massive imposter syndrome when signing their first few clients
- Spends minimal time on outreach, gets distracted by low-value busywork (e.g. building a website) while
  knowing it's a delusion
- Primarily driven by one word: freedom (financial, location, time)
- Blames most business problems on their niche choice
- Puts $10,000/month on a pedestal as the ultimate goal
- Uses the agency to channel an unusual amount of conscientious energy
- Started the agency out of anger, or terror of mediocrity

**Action item:** list 5-10 latent conditions that exist in a client's specific market before writing copy
for them. If none come to mind, the research isn't deep enough yet - see `wiki/list-building.md` and
`clients/{slug}/icp.md` for where that research should already live.

---

## 13. Final Synthesis

The market has a current situation and a desired one. The job of the offer is to position the product as
the vehicle between the two. In practice that means: reverse the risk, make the outcome tangible, focus on
the outcome over the mechanism, solve a real problem, and communicate all of it as simply as possible.

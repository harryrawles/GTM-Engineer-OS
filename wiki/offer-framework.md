# Offer Framework

12-step process for building and validating an offer before writing copy. Run through this when launching a new campaign or when an existing offer is underperforming.

> **The theory behind this process** - why pain must be amplified indirectly, why confidence has three
> sources, why raising price can raise perceived value, why "the gap" matters - lives in
> [wiki/offer-creation-theory.md](./offer-creation-theory.md). Read it if a step below feels arbitrary.

Every "you/your" below is the **client**, this builds the client's offer for their own product, not
Instantly's or GTME's pricing (that's the AM's, `sops/am-gtme-responsibility-split.md`).

---

## Step 1: Define Who

- Which niche are you in?
- Which specific problem do you solve for this niche?
- What are the goals of the members of your niche facing this problem?
- What are the consequences of facing this problem?
- What have they tried to do to solve this problem in the past?
- What is their current situation?
- What is their desired situation?
- What do they truly want, in their heart of hearts?
- What do they believe?
- What do they think?
- What do you know to be true about their lives that you can use in your messaging?

**Conclusion:** Write a one-paragraph summary of who you are targeting and what is driving them.

---

## Step 2: Define Outcome

The outcome you promise may be their desired situation, or fixing one or more problems that contribute to it. The outcome should align with what your niche wants, not what you can do.

- What is the big promise you intend on keeping? What is the bold claim?
- How can you make this promise tangible? (numbers-based)
- Where will someone be after you solve their problem?
- How will they feel once they have this outcome?
- What is the end state they will be in after working with you?
- What will this mean to them? What doors does it open?
- Why is this outcome important to them?
- What unconscious drives does this outcome accomplish?
- How does this outcome move them closer to their desired situation?
- What will their next desired state be once the first one is achieved?

**Conclusion:** State the outcome in one sentence, with a specific number attached.

---

## Step 3: Define Timeframe

- What is a realistic timeframe for you to achieve the outcome by?
- Why does it take this long?
- How could you make the timeframe shorter?

**Conclusion:** State the timeframe. Shorter is better for the offer - but only if it is honest.

---

## Step 4: Establish Methodology

- What steps need to be followed for the outcome to be achieved?
- What are the core components of your product?
- How do these components help achieve the outcome?
- List what needs to be done in full detail, in order
- What are the most important items on this list?
- What are the least important items? Can they be removed while still achieving the outcome?
- Rewrite each step in 6-9 words maximum
- Can these steps be understood by a 10-year-old?

---

## Step 5: Create Factors of Value

Predict what problems, obstacles, or objections will arise from your methodology. Engineer solutions.

For each step in the methodology:
- What problems, obstacles, or objections might arise?
- What is the solution to each one?
- Can the solution be explained simply enough for a 10-year-old?

Each factor of value is a proof point that lowers perceived risk and increases willingness to act.

---

## Step 6: Risk Reversal

- Do you guarantee that if someone follows your methodology, they will achieve the outcome?
- If not, why not? There are probably further factors of value to create.
- If yes, build out the guarantee:
  - Will you offer a refund?
  - Are you willing to go in the red if it doesn't work?
  - Will you work for free if it doesn't work?
  - What could you give them as an apology if it doesn't work?
  - Are you willing to work on a performance basis?
  - How are you aligning incentives?

**State the guarantee.** The bolder the better.

---

## Step 7: Polarise

- Who can't you help?
- Who is the outcome not for?
- What mindset would someone need for you to NOT work with them?

Polarisation makes the offer more compelling for the right person. Trying to appeal to everyone dilutes it.

---

## Step 8: Ultimatum

Set a contrast between upside and downside. Infinite upside, very little downside.

- **Best case:** What is the best possible outcome if they take you up on the offer?
- **Worst case:** What is the absolute worst that happens?

The gap between best and worst case is your offer's power. Make it obvious.

---

## Step 9: Packaging (Positioning)

Re-position your product against market assumptions.

- If you brought it back to bare bones, what are you actually selling?
- How do you make this sound aligned with what they want, in as few words as possible?

The first answer likely positions you the same as everyone else. The second answer is your positioning.

---

## Step 10: Pricing

- What is your competition charging for a similar outcome?
- What is the outcome worth to someone?
- What are they willing to pay?
- At which point does price exceed value?
- What do you think the outcome is worth in monetary terms?
- What are you going to charge?
- If you 2x'd your price, what value would you need to add to justify it?
- If you 5x'd your price, what value would you need to add?
- If you 10x'd your price?

---

## Step 11: Short-form Message

Write a short-form version of the offer (cold email, cold LinkedIn message). Under 100 words.

Apply the 4-part formula from wiki/copywriting-101.md:
1. Personalisation
2. Who Am I
3. The Offer
4. CTA

---

## Step 12: Long-form Message

Write a long-form version for longer-form contexts (proposals, sales follow-up, landing pages).

Apply the TIPS framework from wiki/copywriting-101.md:
1. Trigger
2. Implication
3. Pain
4. Social Proof
5. Solution
6. Soft CTA

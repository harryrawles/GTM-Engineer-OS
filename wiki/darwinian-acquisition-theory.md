# Iterative Darwinian Acquisition

The biological model underneath the OS's testing discipline: client acquisition systems behave like
living systems, and the scientific method is how you deliberately steer their evolution instead of leaving
it to chance.

**Important - read this before assuming anything here is missing elsewhere:** the "God's Tool: The
Scientific Method" section of this theory (formulate → test → observe → iterate hypothesis, Ceteris
Paribus, needle-mover variables, modification levels, V-numbering, the regression check) is **already
fully built out in `wiki/scientific-method.md`**, including the same worked examples (the Content-CTA-
Delivery video genotype, the 1% ABR → $100k ARR scaling math). This file does not repeat that process. It
supplies the biological reasoning *underneath* it - why genotypes, fitness, and variation are the right

that aren't in that file.

## 1. The Biology Primer

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Common descent** | All species trace back to a shared ancestor |
| **Descent with modification** | Offspring differ slightly from parents; enough generations of this produces a new species from a common ancestor |
| **Natural selection** | Organisms with traits better adapted to their environment survive and reproduce more, passing those heritable traits on |
| **Fitness** | Whether an organism survives and reproduces - determined by how well its traits suit its environment |
| **Traits** | Distinctive characteristics that differentiate an organism from others in the same environment (e.g. a finch's beak shape) |
| **DNA / genotype** | The complete genetic code that determines an organism's traits |
| **Variation** | Offspring vary slightly in genotype from their parents (via random mutation) - the raw material natural selection acts on |

**Why variation matters:** a species without variation can't adapt when its environment changes, and is
more vulnerable to extinction. Variation - produced by random mutation - is what lets natural selection
have anything to select from.

**The chain:** `Scale ← Reproduction ← Fitness ← Adaptability ← Traits ← Genotype ← Variation`. Read
right to left: variation produces different genotypes, genotypes produce different traits, traits
determine adaptability to the environment, adaptability determines fitness, fitness determines
reproduction, and successful reproduction is what scales a species.

## 3. Stimulus Genotypes (DNA)

A stimulus is a string of variables, the same way a genotype is a string of genes:

```
Variable 1 + Variable 2 + Variable 3 + Variable 4 = Stimulus
```

A **variable** is any component of a stimulus that can be changed. Variables are the actual building
blocks - this is the level `wiki/scientific-method.md` Step 1.1 asks you to define before anything else.

**Genotype reference - common stimulus types and their key variables:**

| Stimulus | Genotype |
|----------|----------|
| Loom / video cold email | Email copy + Loom script + Delivery |
| Sales pitch | Content + Conviction + Offer |
| Facebook ad | Creative + Copy + Headline + Audience |
| YouTube video | Hook + Content + CTA |
| Sales rebuttal | Empathy + Argument + Conviction |
| YouTube thumbnail | Title + Face + Design |
| Cold email | Subject + Preview + Body copy + CTA (the canonical 4-part formula - see `wiki/copywriting-101.md`) |

Most stimuli have far more than 4 variables in theory, but only 2-4 actually move the needle. Before
diagnosing "my ads don't work," identify which variable - not "the ad" as a whole - is the actual failure
point. Nothing in client acquisition simply "does or doesn't work"; only a specific combination of
variables does or doesn't.

## 5. Traits & Adaptability

Traits are the adjectives that describe a stimulus's nature: long, short, permission-based, clickbaity,
bait-and-switch, transparent, humorous, serious, urgent, captivating, ironic, nonchalant, memorable, dry,
mysterious, passive, aggressive, punchy, subtle, playful, original, emotional, formal, informal, familiar,
calm, friendly, simple, complicated, alarming, offbeat, commanding, shocking, honest, direct, boring -
among many more.

**Traits are not good or bad in isolation.** They're only good or bad relative to whether the humans with
the right latent conditions in a specific niche act on them. Two rules that follow from this:

1. **Traits can be contradictory and both work.** Funny and serious, formal and informal, long and short
   can all succeed *within the same niche*, depending on execution. There is no universally "correct"
   trait - only traits that resonate with a specific niche's psychology.
2. **A handful of traits tend to be near-universal** (transparency, confidence) - but even these need the
   right specific execution. Being "funny and simple" makes action more *likely*, not guaranteed; overdoing
   either trait can undo the benefit.

**Traits decay the same way stimuli do.** A trait that gets great results today may not in a year - the
niche's collective psychology shifts. This is `wiki/first-principles-client-acquisition.md` Section 4
(Stimuli Entropy) applied specifically at the trait level, not just the whole-stimulus level.

**Traits are pattern-based** - if a trait works in one stimulus (a cold call script), it usually
transfers to another (an ad) for the same niche. This is the basis for Stimulus Splicing (Section 10).

## 6. Fitness & Key Variables - a worked example

A stimulus with variables producing traits (and specific execution) conducive to action has **fitness** -
measured by whether it hits KPI, and by how much.

> **Loom cold email, minimum KPIs:** LVR 15% (sent → viewed), PRR 1% (viewed → reply, ~7% of viewers -
> PRR is always measured against total sends, not against views), ABR 3.5% (positive reply → booking,
> ~70% of positive repliers). These are guidelines, not universal - some clients scale on a 40% LVR,
> others on a 1.5% ABR.

| Metric | Variables it measures |
|--------|------------------------|
| LVR | Subject line, body copy, offer, Loom thumbnail, Loom title, follow-up emails |
| PRR | Loom script, Loom delivery, case studies, CTA |
| ABR | Everything upstream of PRR + follow-up process, omnichannel follow-up, reply style |

That's up to 12 variables feeding one Polaris metric - too many to test at once. The **key variables**
(the ones that actually move fitness) here are: **Offer, Body copy, Loom script, Loom delivery.** A boring
Loom title can stay within KPI; a wrong offer cannot be compensated for by anything downstream.

**Diagnostic logic, reading the funnel top to bottom:**
- **ABR (Polaris) in KPI** → the whole genotype (offer-copy-script-delivery) has fitness. Don't touch it.
- **LVR in KPI, PRR not** → the fault is downstream of LVR's variables - most likely script or delivery,
  since they're upstream of what drives a reply.
- **LVR and PRR in KPI, ABR not** → isolate the ABR-specific variables (follow-up process, omnichannel
  touch, reply style) - the core message is fine, the conversion mechanics after a positive reply aren't.

Once the genotype hits fitness on the Polaris metric, you have a DNA string that's provably ready to scale
(see `wiki/acquisition-systems-theory.md` Section 9).

## 7. Playing God

Organisms in nature can't engineer their own genotype - natural selection is blind, slow, and driven by
random mutation over generations. We aren't. Every stimulus we build is a genotype we can *deliberately*
edit and re-test, the same way farmers use artificial selection to breed desirable traits into crops far
faster than nature would alone.

This is the reasoning behind why `wiki/scientific-method.md`'s iteration discipline works at all: it isn't
guesswork dressed up as rigor, it's compressed, directed evolution. Nature needs thousands of generations
for a trait to dominate a population; a deliberate, well-run test can validate or kill a variable in a
single sample-size cycle.

---

## 8. Cause & Effect

The entire testing discipline exists to establish one thing: which specific stimulus (cause) reliably
produces which specific action (effect) - `stimulus genotype exposed to a human with the right latent
conditions → action, moving them to the next stage of the funnel`. Ceteris Paribus (holding every other
variable constant - already the operating law in `wiki/scientific-method.md` Step 1.7) is what makes this
possible: change more than one variable and the cause-effect link between any single variable and the
result becomes unknowable, and the entire test's data is worthless.

## 9. Common Descent & Darwinian Stimuli, Applied

Your V1 of any stimulus is the **common ancestor** every later version descends from - provided it has
enough heritable fitness to be worth iterating rather than discarding outright. Every subsequent version
(V2, V3...) is a new generation, and given enough generations of disciplined iteration
(`wiki/scientific-method.md` Step 4), the stimulus evolves toward the most adapted version achievable for
that niche. This is the biological justification for the strict V-numbering already mandatory in
`clients/{slug}/test-log.md` - the version history *is* the evolutionary record.

**Random mutated variation:** variation is what makes evolution possible at all - without it, a species
(or a stimulus) can't adapt when the environment shifts. Don't just reuse this OS's proven variables
verbatim; introduce deliberate or even random variation on top of them. If genuinely stuck, borrow a random
input from somewhere unrelated (a line from an unrelated book, an idea from an unrelated discipline - see
`wiki/first-principles-client-acquisition.md` Section 6, Multi-Disciplinary Thinking) and test whether it
applies. The best-performing systems are rarely copy-pasted; they're the result of relentless variation on
top of a working base.

**Petri dish tests** (duplicating a working system into an identical environment to test improvements
without risking the live version) are already fully specified in `wiki/acquisition-systems-theory.md`
Sections 6 and 10 - same concept, no restatement needed here.

## 10. Initial Conditions - the Butterfly Effect

Complex systems are extremely sensitive to their starting conditions - small early differences compound
into large ones over time. `wiki/acquisition-systems-theory.md` Section 12 already lists the Butterfly
Effect as a supporting model; this is the fuller illustration:

> Two ships leave southern Argentina both aiming for Australia, differing in initial heading by just 1
> degree - under 0.3% of a full circle. Ship 1 docks in South Perth. Ship 2 docks in West Tasmania - a
> 2,850km difference, purely from that 1-degree gap compounding over the full voyage.

**Applied to acquisition systems:** the closer a V1 genotype is to fitness from the start, the better the
long-run outcome - because the gap between "close" and "off" compounds with every subsequent generation of
iteration, the same way 1 degree compounds into 2,850km over distance (or time, in a system). A niche
understood 5% better than competitors understand it isn't a marginal edge - over enough iterations, it's
the difference between docking in the right country.

## 11. Advanced Genetic Ideology

Three techniques beyond single-variable iteration, once a genotype has proven fitness:

### Stimulus Splicing

Splicing, in genetics, means inserting a gene that produces a desirable trait from one organism's DNA into
another's. Applied here: once a variable (or the trait it produces) is proven to work in one stimulus,
transplant it into other stimuli - even in unrelated systems. *Example: a niche responds well to
transparency in YouTube content → splice "transparency" as a trait into cold email copy, sales scripts,
and ad creative, not just the original video.*

### System Cross-Pollination

Cross-pollination combines the DNA of two varieties into a new one. Applied here: if two different
stimulus genotypes are each independently proven to have fitness in two different systems, deliberately
combine elements of both into a new hybrid stimulus and test whether the combination outperforms either
parent.

### Network Effect Conjugation

Bacterial conjugation is how bacteria share fitness-producing DNA directly with each other via a plasmid -
a donor with a useful gene transfers it to a recipient that lacks it, and the recipient instantly gains
that fitness. Applied to a community of operators: when a winning variable combination is shared rather
than hoarded, and enough operators do this, a self-sustaining network effect emerges - a shared pool of
winning genotypes than makes every participant's iteration cycle faster than the equivalent iteration
running in isolation.

**This OS already implements Network Effect Conjugation structurally** - see `CLAUDE.md` → "The
Compounding Loop (the OS as a living organism)". `gtm-skills/weekly-reviewer.md` is the conjugation
mechanism: it takes a de-identified, proven-fit pattern from one client (the donor) and transfers it into
`wiki/`, `frameworks/`, or `best-practices/` (the shared plasmid pool), where every other client (potential
recipients) can inherit that fitness without having to independently discover it. Every client onboarded
into this OS is, in effect, already inside a conjugation network the moment they're added.

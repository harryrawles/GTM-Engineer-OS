## 1. Do the Math

Best when you can put numbers to the problem.

**Structure:** Trigger (with number) → quick pitch → back-of-napkin calculation → CTA.

```
Mary, noticed you have over 50 open positions on your job portal.

We help tech scale-ups reduce new employee churn from 30% to 10%.

With cost of mis-hire around $30,000, this would mean going from 15 mis-hires to 5 - $300,000 saved.

Worth a chat?
```

## 2. Short Trigger-Based Outreach

Best for quick, punchy, under-30-word emails.

**Structure:** Relevant trigger → validation + value prop → CTA.

```
G, saw that Reach's latest blog post does not include a Meta tag - affects online visibility.

{{client}} improved theirs by creating meta tags using ChatGPT.

Can I share how?
```

---

## 3. Challenge of Similar Companies

Best when the industry has a known, widespread pain point.

**Structure:** Personalisation → challenge others in the industry face → solution → CTA → PS with something funny.

```
Hey Patrick,

We work with security officers at banks facing losses of $9K per hour from ransomware, with phishing victims every 11 seconds.

We have a global team available 24/7 to respond and contain cyber incidents in hours, not days. When did you last test your plan?

Cheers,
P.

PS: I would attach a link but we both work in security.
```

---

## 4. Neutral Insight

Best when you have a relevant third-party resource to share.

**Structure:** Reference a third-party resource → explain why they should read it → why you are sharing it → soft ask.

```
George, do you read Outreach's blog?

Given you are likely ramping reps, I thought you would find it interesting.

The VP of Sales Dev at Segment wrote about scaling her team to a $3.2B acquisition. Without canned templates.

Check it out.

Will

PS: Any thoughts on my last note?
```

---

## 5. Leader Responsibilities

Best for reaching role-specific leaders with curiosity.

**Structure:** "As a {{role}} leader, curious how {{responsibility}}" → contextual offer → personalise.

```
Armand, as a Sales leader, curious how your reps prioritise which accounts to reach out to?

If I created a list of accounts researching competitors like Salesforce, would it be worth a conversation?

Either way, congrats on being named Top Sales Coach.
```

---

## 6. Ask Before Pitch / Pattern Interrupt

Flips the typical cold email by leading with a question.

**Structure:** Open-ended question → observation → potential problem → how product solves it → PS for relevance.

```
Hey Will, think this would help George and Anne?

You are hiring new sellers. Despite the same templates, some reps get results and others do not.

Lavender gives you a clearer picture on why. We have seen it identify clear personalisation plays for the team.

Figured that would be relevant with the new focus upmarket.

Let me know.
```

---

## 7. Not Too Different Persona

Best when you can reference peers with the same problem.

**Structure:** "I work with {{similar ICP}} → they tell me they struggle with X."

Position yourself as someone who already understands their world because you work with people just like them.

---

## 8. Upfront Value

Lead with free value, no pitch.

**Structure:** "[Here is value offering] - hope it was helpful."

```
Here is a list of every founder at every company that just implemented HubSpot. Hope it was helpful.
```

---

## 9. Leverage Content in Outbound

Use a lead magnet as the entry point.

**Structure:** "[Content name] [how it helps] → can I send it over? → PS for relevance."

```
Hey Jason - we compiled a cheat sheet on how teams like {{social_proof}} are getting AEs self-sourcing 30%+ of their pipeline.

Can I send it over?

Ethan

PS: Thought this was relevant because I saw you recently decreased SDR headcount and are hiring 3 more AEs.
```

---

## 10. "Why Are You Paying?"

Analogy-based persuasion.

**Structure:** Set the stage with a typical life situation → describe an obvious choice → compare it to the choice the prospect is making.

Make the prospect realise they are overpaying or overworking for something that should be simple - through a relatable analogy, not a pitch.

## 11. Write a Good Cold Email (Josh Braun)

Problem-first framework. The thinking process before writing:

1. Who are you targeting?
2. What problem are you trying to solve?
3. What are they currently doing to solve it?
4. What is the problem with their current solution?
5. How does your solution solve it differently?

**Rules:**
- Be crispy and specific.
- Stay away from generic words ("optimise", "streamline", "save time", "save money").
- Focus on what sucks about how they currently get the job done - not your value prop. Solutions have no value without a problem people can relate to.

```
James, honestly now - did you spend your youth dreaming about hard-pasting Excel pages into Google Sheets and manually adjusting payouts, one sheet at a time?
```

## 12. HOT Outreach Template

Three-section structure.

**Section 1 - Peak Interest (the problem you found):**
- Say the problem you found ("Your website is slow")
- Mention something not obvious ("I listened to what you said in X")
- Anything related ("I also love X place")

**Section 2 - The Value (what makes you an expert):**
Give them the answer. Whatever you want them to pay you for, give them something right now.
- "If you change your email to long form, you will easily get a 20% open rate difference."
- "I re-edited a video of yours - here it is and why I did XYZ."

**Section 3 - About You and CTA:**
- I am X from Y
- I have done Z
- I think I can help you here
- **Always end with a question** - never "Let me know" or "Let us get a call"

## 13. Manual Email Style (Josh Braun's Henry email)

Casual, human, low-pressure.

```
Hey Josh - subscribed to your email list a few weeks ago. Been doing digital marketing for 8 years - have a few unconventional ideas that might goose sales of your Badass Guide without offering discounts.

I do not get paid unless you make more.

Worth batting around a few ideas? Henry.

PS: Not sure it is a fit, but several course creators I work with are seeing a 10-12% MoM boost in sales.
```

**Why it works:**
- "Subscribed" - personalisation
- "Do not get paid unless you make more" - lowers zone of resistance
- "Not sure it is a fit" - also lowers ZOR, sounds curious not pushy
- "10-12% increase" - plausible and exciting social proof
- "A few unconventional ideas" - piques curiosity
- Casual writing - feels more relatable

## ColdIQ Playbook - Insights from 250K+ Emails

### 3 Preview / First Line Patterns

#### Type 1 - Observation (general triggers)
Call out something you noticed, without explaining why. Could mean: potential customer, question, or research.
```
Hey John, saw you offer a free audit on your site.
```

#### Type 2 - Pain (specific triggers)
Reference a pain point you are confident they have. Connect the trigger to the why.
```
Bad: "Noticed you are hiring a BDR."
Better: "Hey John, here is a top-of-funnel outbound playbook. With {{company}} hiring for a BDR I assume generating top of funnel is a priority."
```

#### Type 3 - Industry (enterprise / regulated)
Hot topic they have seen, been impacted by, or regulation change.
```
Subject: tariffs
Preview: Hey John, with cost rising on imports from China, are you considering your options?
```

### 3 Value Proposition Styles

#### Style 1 - Show them the cost of the problem
**Pattern:** Observation/pain → here is what that is costing you → specific number.

```
Hey John, most businesses at the 5-year mark are leaving $50K-$200K in available capital on the table - typically because they do not know what they qualify for.

Based on your revenue range, {{company}} likely qualifies for more than you would expect.

Open to hearing more?
```

#### Style 2 - Peer proof ("Others like you are doing X")
**Pattern:** Observation/pain → other companies like you are doing X → result they are getting.

```
Hey John, congrats on crossing the 5-year mark. A lot of businesses at your stage are using growth capital to fund expansion without touching cash reserves.

One owner in {{industry}} locked in $150K at terms that let him keep full ownership.

Useful for {{company}}?
```

#### Style 3 - Show a specific outcome
**Pattern:** Observation/pain → specific outcome → want to see if it applies?

```
Hey John, since you just opened a second location, I imagine managing cash flow across both is a priority.

We helped a {{industry}} business secure $200K in growth capital in under 2 weeks - no equity, no collateral.

Make sense to see what {{company}} qualifies for?
```

### CTA Patterns

**Interest-based (safest at scale):**
- "Think this could help your team?"
- "Interested to learn more?"
- "Worth exploring?"

**Value-first / resource:**
- "Want me to send over a quick breakdown?"
- "I put together a comparison for businesses your size - want me to share it?"

**Routing / right person:**
- "Are you the right person that handles {{responsibility}} at {{company}}?"
- "Would this sit with you or someone else on the team?"

### ColdIQ Principles (from 250K+ emails)

1. **Write for the 97%.** Optimise for the majority who will not reply, not the 3% who will.
2. **Keep it tight.** See wiki/copywriting-101.md for canonical word count (ATL 60 / BTL 90 / default 75).
3. **2-step sequences work best.** Do not over-sequence.
4. **13x gap** between worst and best email variants - testing matters enormously.
5. **Subject + preview = complete thought.** They work together.
6. **Position yourself as a potential customer** in the subject line.
7. **Soft CTAs > time asks.** "Think this could help?" beats "Got 15 min?"
8. **Segment > individual personalisation at scale.** Effort-to-output ratio is better.
9. **Preview line IS the first line.** First 50 characters appear in inbox.

### ColdIQ Pain-Point Angles (from 73 prospect calls)

When selling outbound / GTM services:
1. **Referral ceiling** - "How do you generate pipeline beyond referrals?"
2. **Failed DIY outbound** - "You tried outbound, it did not work."
3. **System vs leads** - "You need a system, not just more leads."
4. **Scale without headcount** - "Grow pipeline without hiring more reps."

## Josh Braun - The 5 Writing Principles

1. **Write with an eraser.** Remove every unnecessary word.
2. **Be cheeky.** Personality matters. Make them smile.
3. **Be specific.** Credibility comes through precision. "47% increase" beats "significant improvement."
4. **Do not say "we reduce costs."** Say "e-commerce companies doing $5k/month overpaying 10-15%."
5. **Use loss aversion.** Frame around what they are losing, not gaining. People act more to avoid loss than to gain.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Long subject lines (7+ words)
- Misleading clickbait
- Same copy for every persona
- All caps or spammy words ("FREE", "OFFER")
- Making it about YOU not them
- "Helping {{company}} with growth" - too obviously a pitch
- "Quick call?" - overused, screams cold email

---

## A/B Testing Reminders

- Test 3 subject lines per campaign
- Track reply rate to meeting rate, not just opens
- Match inbox type to prospect (Gmail to Gmail, Outlook to Outlook)
- Run the "So What?" test on every signal: is it recent? Does it connect to my offer? Would they care?
